# Data decisions behind the Olly Threads launch

I wanted the launch decisions to come from numbers, not from whatever sounded smart in the moment.

So I used real TikTok Analytics and Instagram Insights data, cleaned it into CSVs, and tied the biggest product calls back to measurable behavior.

## Data sources

- TikTok Analytics captured June 15, 2026
- Instagram Insights captured June 14, 2026
- TikTok followers: 201,851
- TikTok viewers in the 30-day window: 2.5 million
- Instagram accounts reached: 1,221,538
- Instagram profile visits: 124,733
- Instagram interactions: 1,029,391
- Instagram net followers in the window: 31,440

I only used printed values from the analytics screens. If a number was cut off or only visible as a line chart shape, I did not guess it.

## Decision 1: ship in the United States first

The United States had 14.1% of TikTok viewers but 25.8% of TikTok followers. That creates a 1.83x follow-conversion index.

That matters because followers are a stronger signal than viewers. Viewers can be accidental. Followers show intent.

Brazil was also interesting at 1.05x, so I would watch it closely. But at launch, the clean decision was US first. Domestic shipping means fewer customs issues, fewer support problems, and a better chance of learning from real orders without overcomplicating fulfillment on day one.

What would change my mind: if Brazil crosses 10% of followers or if Brazilian visitors repeatedly hit the checkout wall.

## Decision 2: design for followers, not viewers

I expected the buyer to be mostly male. That was my assumption.

But the follower data pushed back. TikTok viewers were almost even: 50% male, 49% female. Followers were different: 40% male, 60% female.

And followers matter more because they are closer to buyers.

So the launch should not blindly follow the default anime-streetwear male buyer assumption. Product photography, size planning, and messaging should speak to the committed audience that is actually there.

What would change my mind: real purchaser data after a few hundred orders.

## Decision 3: price for a younger audience

On TikTok, 86.9% of followers were 18 to 34. Instagram showed 82.2% in the same age range.

That is a strong audience, but it is still price-sensitive. A lot of those people are students or early-career buyers. So the product strategy should not pretend everyone has luxury-brand money.

The launch logic:

- Keep single items under $40 when possible.
- Test free shipping at $75.
- Use the threshold to encourage two-item orders without immediately discounting the brand.

What would change my mind: average order value. If buyers stay far below $75, the threshold is probably too high and should move closer to $60.

## Decision 4: growth content should be process video

The highest-like post was not the best conversion post.

The Goku speed paint earned 34.1 follows per 1,000 likes. The biggest static TikTok filters post earned 16.7 follows per 1,000 likes.

That is a big gap. Process content made people care more. They watched the art being made, not just the finished image.

So product drops should ride process videos. Static art can still support the feed, but speed paints should carry launch announcements.

What would change my mind: repeated sales data showing a different content format produces more buyers, not just more followers.

## The bigger point

This is how I tried to manage the project: make the call, write down why, and write down what would make me reverse it.

That keeps decisions honest. And it keeps the team from defending old guesses after the data changes.
